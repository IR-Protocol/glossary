# Subscription Model Does Not Fit AI — A Game of Mismatch

## One-Liner
The subscription model works for software with near-zero marginal cost, but AI has real, variable compute costs — locking platforms, light users, and heavy users into a losing game.

## Context
Have you ever paid for an AI membership, used it only a few times, and then suddenly seen a message: “Your quota is exhausted. Please wait a few hours to continue.” Your first reaction: “I paid — why am I being restricted?” It’s not bad luck. The subscription model, applied to AI, simply does not work. With software, marginal cost is almost zero. But every AI query burns electricity and GPU cycles — real, variable costs. Light users feel they overpay; heavy users get throttled the most; platforms struggle to survive. No one wins.

## Insight
Subscription pricing is structurally flawed for AI: revenue is fixed, but costs are floating. Platforms have no choice but to throttle heavy users — the very people who rely on AI most. This creates a perverse cycle: the more you use, the more you are pushed away. The problem is not optimization — it is the model itself. The solution is to decouple storage from compute. Storage (memory space) works like rent — a fixed monthly fee. Compute (each query’s token consumption) works like electricity — pay as you go. This “dual-track” approach aligns incentives, removes hidden throttling, and makes pricing transparent and fair for all.

## Implication
When storage and compute are separated, light users stop subsidizing heavy users, heavy users pay for what they actually use without arbitrary caps, and platforms can scale sustainably. AI becomes a utility — predictable, fair, and no longer a source of bill shock or silent restrictions. This is not a pricing tweak; it is a structural shift.
