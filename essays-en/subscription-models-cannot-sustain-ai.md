# Subscription Models Cannot Sustain AI: A Mismatch of Fixed Revenue vs. Variable Costs

## One-Liner

The AI subscription model is structurally broken — it locks revenue while costs spiral, and no amount of pricing tiers can fix a fundamental mismatch between fixed income and variable expenses.

## Context

Ever had this happen?

You pay for an AI subscription, use it lightly for a few days, and suddenly a message pops up: "Daily quota exhausted. Please wait a few hours."

Your first thought: *I paid for this — why am I being throttled?*

You're not unlucky. Subscriptions simply don't fit AI's underlying cost structure.

Here's why: subscriptions charge a flat monthly fee. But AI costs are anything but fixed.

Some users ask a few questions a day. Others run projects, write code, build solutions — chatting for hours at a time.

Both pay the same monthly fee. Yet the compute cost the platform incurs for each can vary by dozens or even hundreds of times.

That creates an awkward triangle.

Light users feel: *I'm overpaying for what I actually use.*

Heavy users feel: *Why am I, your most loyal customer, being throttled the hardest?*

Platforms feel: *We're bleeding money if this continues.*

Nobody wins.

## Insight

So platforms push back — they have no choice.

They can't let everyone use the service freely, or costs will spiral out of control.

So they throttle. They cap conversations, slow down response times, limit usage duration.

The more you depend on the service, the more it pushes you away.

Heavy users should be the platform's most valuable customers. Under the subscription model, they become a burden instead.

Does that make sense?

AI is not software. It's electricity.

Software subscriptions worked when marginal costs were near zero. Every extra second you used a piece of software didn't cost the vendor anything extra.

AI is different.

Every question you ask burns electricity, spins up GPUs, consumes compute.

It's not software — it's a utility.

Have you ever seen "unlimited electricity for a flat monthly fee"?

No. Because if that existed, heavy users would bankrupt the power grid.

AI is no different.

Here's a more brutal number: one leading AI product's daily token consumption has surpassed 120 trillion, doubling every three months. No fixed monthly fee can keep pace with that growth. Revenue hits a ceiling while costs grow exponentially. This is not a strategy problem — it's a structural financial flaw.

Subscriptions are not merely "not good enough." They are unsustainable.

No matter how many tiers you add, no matter how many membership levels you design, the math never balances as long as revenue is fixed and costs are variable. Optimizing a broken structure just gives you a more polished failure. The flaw is not in the execution; it's in the model itself.

Some might argue: why not adjust pricing, add more tiers?

But that's patching a shirt when you need a new one — at best a temporary fix, at worst a guarantee that you'll keep hitting the same wall.

The root problem is structural: fixed revenue, variable costs.

As long as that structure stands, platforms must limit users, and users must tolerate quotas and interruptions.

This isn't something you can optimize away. It's a model error. And model errors can only be replaced, not fixed.

## Implication

What if we took a different approach?

Keep two things separate: **storage** and **compute**.

Storage is your memory space. Rent it monthly, like paying rent.

Compute is the processing power behind every conversation. Pay for what you use, like electricity.

Once decoupled, heavy users pay for their actual consumption — platforms don't need to throttle anyone in secret. Light users aren't subsidising others. Everyone knows where they stand.

This is not about tweaking prices. It's about fundamentally balancing the math of AI services from the ground up.

For users, it means no more guessing whether you'll hit a cap mid-project. For platforms, it means serving heavy users without going bankrupt. For the industry, it means AI can finally evolve from a toy into a sustainable business.
