# DAA Points in the Right Direction — What Comes Next? A Parallel Reflection on Measuring AI Value

*We are Intention Resonance Technology. We write this because we have spent the past two months building a framework for AI commercialization — first in our White Paper 1.0 (April 2026), then in White Paper 2.0 (May 2026). When we saw Robin Li propose DAA at Baidu Create 2026, we knew we had to respond — not as critics, but as fellow explorers on the same road.*

In May 2026, at Baidu Create, Robin Li introduced a new concept: DAA (Daily Active Agents).

In short: DAA does not measure "how many people use AI" or "how much compute is consumed." It measures "how many AI agents are actually doing real work for people." Its core proposition is clear: in the AI era, value should be measured by output, not input.

Is this direction right? Yes.

The AI industry does need to shift from "compute consumed" to "output created." On that point, the thinking is ahead of its time.

But the framework built by the *AI Memory Chain* White Papers 1.0 and 2.0 offers a different lens through which to examine DAA. Standing on this framework, a different set of questions emerges.

This article is not a critique, nor a correction. It is a parallel reflection — looking at how AI value should be measured from another angle, and asking whether there might be another path.

## What DAA Gets Right — and What It Actually Measures

DAA's logic is clear: agents doing work means value is being created.

But here is the question: what counts as "active"?

Does an agent that automatically sends weather updates every day count as "active"? Does an agent built by a developer just to run tests count? Does a bot wrapped in generic prompts — created, run through a few script cycles, then abandoned — count?

These are not technical questions. They are questions of definition.

When a metric is established as an industry standard, it inevitably faces pressure to be inflated. DAU had its share of fake accounts and script bots, but later metrics like retention and time spent could cross-validate it. For DAA, what validates "completed useful tasks"? User confirmation? API callback data? Platform self-certification?

If the platform acts as both player and referee, the statistical standard becomes — shall we say — a test of discipline.

This is not to question anyone's integrity. What is worth thinking about is a more fundamental question:

DAA measures "the number of active tools on a platform." But what users really care about is "whether my AI belongs to me."

These two things may not be moving in the same direction.

## Agent Prosperity ≠ User Ownership

There is an important distinction to make here.

The "agents" measured by DAA are, by nature, task-oriented tools — created to do specific jobs: write copy, schedule production, handle customer service. They belong to the platform and serve efficiency.

But the deeper need of ordinary users may not be "give me more tools." It might be "give me a partner that understands me better over time."

The difference between these two is clearly reflected in their business models:

**Agent platforms** — logic is traffic distribution. The more an agent is used, the more exposure it gets, and the platform benefits.
**Personal AI** — logic is asset custody. Users pay for storage and management of their digital memory.

One is "helping you find good tools." The other is "helping you protect what is yours."

DAA can measure the first. It cannot measure the second.

## A Historical Echo: Will the Agent Square Become the Next WeChat Official Accounts?

In 2012, WeChat Official Accounts launched with the slogan: "Every individual, no matter how small, has their own brand." Free registration. Anyone could start one.

A decade later:

Most people never started one — they didn't know what to write.
Most of those who started abandoned them. Only professional creators and commercial entities sustained operations.
For ordinary people, Official Accounts were not "my space" — they were "someone else's media."
Today's agent creation platforms are replaying the same script:

**B端 won't use them** — Enterprises don't want to build a chatbot on a public square. They want to embed AI into their ERP, customer service, and supply chain systems.
**C端 won't build them** — Ordinary people lack the ability and motivation to "create" agents. They want something already built that belongs to them.
Only a few are playing the game — prompt engineers and AI tutorial creators. It is a creator ecosystem, not a user ecosystem.

This is not any single company's fault. It is a structural limitation of the "square model" in the AI era: lowering creation barriers does not guarantee ecosystem prosperity. The key is whether you have lowered the barrier to "ownership" for ordinary people.

## Another Direction: Dual-Track and the Digital Companion

The core work of the *AI Memory Chain* White Papers 1.0 and 2.0 is to diagnose the structural misfit of subscriptions in the AI era, and to propose an alternative framework.

Subscriptions are not "not good enough" — they are **unsustainable**. One leading AI product's daily token consumption has surpassed 120 trillion, doubling every three months — no fixed monthly fee can keep pace with that cost curve. Revenue hits a ceiling while costs grow exponentially. The math never balances from the start.

No matter how you optimize tiers, subscriptions only delay the problem. This is not an optimization problem — it is a model error. And model errors can only be replaced, not fixed.

Based on this diagnosis, the White Papers propose the **Dual-Track Model [IR-DT-002]** :

- **Storage rent — like paying rent.** Users rent a private cloud memory space monthly. Conversation history, preferences, project data all live there — client-side encrypted, user holds the key, platform stores only ciphertext. Switch devices, clear cache — memory persists.
- **Token pay-as-you-go — like paying electricity.** Each AI reply consumes compute, billed by actual usage — pay for what you use.

This logic is not new. The telecom industry has done it for decades — monthly plan plus usage-based billing. Users are already used to it.

But what the White Papers offer the industry is not just a billing model — it is a **cognitive framework**. The core work of 2.0 is Cognitive Anchoring [IR-CA-001] — mapping unfamiliar technical concepts to familiar human experiences. Trust issues are anchored to "dashcams" and "black boxes." Graceful degradation [IR-GD-005] is anchored to "phone suspension" — "ting ji bao hao" (a Chinese telecom practice where you temporarily suspend service while keeping your number and contacts).

These anchors all point to the same thing: AI services must become trustworthy infrastructure, not just another software product.

And when an AI remembers your preferences, project context, and decision-making habits — surviving device switches and cache clears — it ceases to be a "tool you use" and begins to become your **Digital Companion [IR-DC-009]** .

Once this relationship is established, user value perception shifts from "what can this AI do for me" to "I cannot lose these memories." Anxiety shifts from "being replaced" to "losing what I have built."

This is true retention. Not lock-in. Attachment.

## Not Competition — Another Possibility

The Memory Chain is model-agnostic. It does not replace any underlying LLM — it sits above as a memory middleware layer, compatible with any major LLM.

This is not competition. This is exploring another possibility: enabling every AI service provider to offer a genuine "digital companion" experience to their users. The relevant technical architecture has been filed for invention patents, but the framework remains open. We welcome industry discussion, reference, and co-exploration.

Version 1.0 established the naming. Version 2.0 drew the map.

This article is a parallel reflection — standing on that map, looking at the industry's latest developments.

## Closing: The Direction Is Clear — The Road Still Needs Building

Robin Li proposed DAA. The direction is right. The AI industry does need a new measure of value.

Could this measure not only count "active tools on platforms," but also measure the depth of "digital companions owned by each person"?

Agents are the platform's prosperity. Personal AI is the individual's capability.

The former measures breadth. The latter measures depth. They are not contradictory.

One path builds AI into a busier square. The other builds AI into a partner that knows you better.

Not choosing the first does not make it wrong. Choosing the second does not make it the only answer.

But as White Paper 2.0 said: exploration has a window. Whoever finds the path first gets to name it.

The AI era will give birth to a new core asset: the **Memory Chain [IR-MC-001]** . Just as the social graph locked human connections, the Memory Chain will lock the accumulation between humans and AI.

Keep the memory, keep the user.
